## 2. New findings

### Reverse engineering should be modeled as a staged sensemaking workflow
The clearest anchor this run was:
- **Votipka et al., An Observational Investigation of Reverse Engineers’ Processes** (USENIX Security 2020)

Its accessible abstract is high signal even without the full paper body. It reports an observational interview study of **16 reverse engineers** and distills a three-phase model:
- **overview**
- **sub-component scanning**
- **focused experimentation**

It also reports a broad phase distinction in method choice:
- static methods dominate more in the first two phases
- dynamic methods become more central in the focused experimentation phase

This is important for the KB because it argues against representing expertise as just a bag of tricks. A better model is a **workflow with phase transitions**, where questions, evidence needs, and useful tool affordances shift over time.

### Malware/reversing workflow taxonomy looks rich enough to stand beside benchmark taxonomies
A second useful lead came from a workflow-oriented malware-analysis study whose PDF extraction was messy, but whose accessible structure already signals a lot.

The table of contents alone suggests stable categories around:
- analyst objectives
- analyst tiers
- prioritization
- main analysis process
- environment setup
- evasion handling
- workflow-difference factors
- usability recommendations

That is enough to justify treating analyst-workflow literature as a real structural family in this KB rather than a loose appendix.

### Human–LLM teaming in SRE is now mature enough to be a first-class subtopic
The strongest directly new paper this run was:
- **Decompiling the Synergy: An Empirical Study of Human–LLM Teaming in Software Reverse Engineering** (NDSS 2026)

The NDSS page reports:
- online survey of **153 practitioners**
- human study with **48 participants**
  - **24 novices**
  - **24 experts**
- over **109 hours** of instrumented SRE
- **18 findings** on benefits and harms

Most important reported results:
- novice comprehension rate improves by about **98%**, roughly matching experts
- experts gain comparatively little
- known-algorithm functions are triaged up to **2.4× faster**
- artifact recovery (**symbols, comments, types**) increases by at least **66%**
- harms include hallucinations, unhelpful suggestions, and ineffective outputs

This suggests a much sharper framing for the KB:
- LLMs are not uniform expert multipliers
- they seem especially good at **narrowing the novice–expert gap**
- they are useful for **artifact bootstrap and triage acceleration**
- but they also introduce a persistent **verification and trust-calibration burden**

### A new distinction is emerging: assistance value vs trust burden
One of the best synthesis points from this run is that analyst-support research should probably track at least two separate dimensions:

- **assistance value**
  - faster triage
  - better comprehension
  - better artifact recovery
  - quicker orientation
- **trust burden**
  - hallucinated semantics
  - overconfident but wrong naming/type suggestions
  - unhelpful tool calls or irrelevant suggestions
  - context loss and prompt-window failure

That distinction feels very important for expert reversing. A tool can improve throughput while still degrading confidence calibration or forcing expensive manual checking.

### Practical LLM usefulness depends heavily on interface/orchestration, not just model quality
The Cisco Talos article:
- **Using LLMs as a reverse engineering sidekick**

adds a practical systems angle that the empirical study alone does not cover.

Useful reported themes include:
- MCP-mediated integration with **IDA Pro** / **Ghidra**-style tooling
- need for models that handle structured instructions and tool use well
- importance of large context windows
- cost inflation from repeated tool-mediated prompts
- privacy/confidentiality tradeoffs for cloud models
- latency and memory constraints for local models

This is structurally important because it suggests that “LLM for RE” should not be treated as one monolithic category. At least three layers matter:
- **model capability**
- **tool/interface integration**
- **analyst verification workflow**

### Cognitive-support literature gives a better vocabulary for expert RE than pure automation framing
The Frontiers 2026 article:
- **Immersive sensemaking for binary reverse engineering: a survey and synthesis**

is not mainly about LLMs, but it was still a valuable addition because it frames binary RE as:
- uncertainty-heavy
- abductive
- working-memory constrained
- dependent on good information organization

Its three support themes are especially worth preserving:
- **enhancing abductive iteration**
- **augmenting working memory**
- **supporting information organization**

That gives the KB a better language for explaining why expert reversing is hard and why certain tools help: they stabilize reasoning and reduce cognitive thrash, not just produce output artifacts.

### Externalization is looking like a hidden core variable
Across the workflow, immersive, and LLM-assistance sources, a recurring pattern is emerging:

Good reversing seems to depend on externalizing:
- hypotheses
- semantic anchors
- naming guesses
- evidence trails
- uncertainty boundaries
- intermediate organizational structure

That implies the KB will probably need future coverage of:
- notebook-style RE workflows
- persistent memory/evidence systems
- annotation and traceability practices
- analyst-facing artifact organization

### A practical experience-gradient hypothesis is emerging
This run suggests a tentative hypothesis worth testing in later research:

- **novices** benefit more from explanation, scaffolding, naming/type suggestions, and algorithm recognition
- **experts** benefit more selectively, perhaps mostly from metadata drafting, search acceleration, and candidate-hypothesis generation
- **experts also pay a sharper cost** for bad suggestions because they may be slowed by verification of plausible-but-wrong output rather than helped by it

That is not yet a settled claim, but it looks like a useful organizing lens.

## 4. Reflections / synthesis
This run strengthened the KB in a way that feels structurally important.

Earlier runs established several evaluation/output-oriented families:
- decompilation evaluation
- symbol/type/signature recovery
- obfuscation/deobfuscation and packed binaries
- firmware/protocol context recovery

This run makes it much clearer that the KB also needs a first-class **analyst-support / workflow family**.

A useful decomposition now looks like:
- **observational studies of reverse engineers**
- **workflow and sensemaking models**
- **human–LLM teaming in reverse engineering**
- **notebook / memory-augmented analysis flows**
- **visualization and immersive-analysis support**
- **automation interfaces shaped by analyst interaction needs**

Another important synthesis is that reverse engineering expertise is looking increasingly like a combination of:
- effective phase transitions in inquiry
- careful externalization of partial understanding
- disciplined evidence management
- trust calibration around automated assistance

That is a better expert model than “strong command of disassemblers” alone.
